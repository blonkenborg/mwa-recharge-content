The conventional advice — sit up straight, don't slouch — misses the point. Research consistently shows that static posture has a weak relationship with back pain. People with "perfect" posture develop back problems. People who sit asymmetrically sometimes don't.

The real variable is muscular capacity.

## What the spine actually needs

The lumbar spine is inherently unstable. It relies on a network of muscles — erector spinae, multifidus, quadratus lumborum, and the deep core — to maintain position under load. When these muscles are weak or deconditioned, the spine compensates with excessive joint loading and passive tissue strain.

This is why desk workers who are otherwise healthy often develop lower back pain. They're not in bad posture — their supporting musculature has simply atrophied from disuse.

## What strength training fixes

**Erector spinae and multifidus** — these deep back muscles are directly trained through hinge movements. Strengthening them reduces passive loading on the lumbar discs.

**Glutes** — weak glutes shift load to the lower back during standing, walking, and lifting. Glute training is one of the most effective interventions for chronic non-specific lower back pain.

**Core** — not crunches, but anti-rotation and anti-extension work that trains the core to stabilise rather than just flex.

## The EGYM machines and back health

The leg press, back extension, and seated row machines on the EGYM circuit directly address the muscle groups most responsible for lumbar stability. Three sessions per week is enough to produce measurable improvements within six to eight weeks.

Strength training doesn't just reduce back pain — it makes the spine more resilient to the demands of sitting, lifting, and daily life.
