In 2009, researchers coined the phrase "sitting disease" after noticing something alarming: people who exercised regularly but sat for extended periods still showed elevated cardiometabolic risk. Exercise was not fully cancelling out the damage of prolonged sitting.

## What happens physiologically

After about 30 minutes of continuous sitting, electrical activity in the leg muscles drops sharply. Lipoprotein lipase — an enzyme that helps process blood fats — decreases by over 90%. Blood glucose regulation worsens. These are not long-term changes; they start within the first hour.

Over months and years, chronic sitting is associated with increased risk of type 2 diabetes, higher blood pressure, reduced lumbar stability, and weaker glutes and hip flexors.

## The exercise paradox

A 2020 meta-analysis in the British Journal of Sports Medicine found that 30-40 minutes of moderate-to-vigorous activity per day largely neutralised the mortality risk from prolonged sitting — but only if the sitting was interrupted. Eight hours of unbroken sitting remained risky even with daily exercise.

The implication: frequency of movement matters as much as total volume.

## What actually helps

1. **Break it up** — stand or walk for 2-3 minutes every 30-45 minutes. This alone restores lipoprotein lipase activity.
2. **Strength train** — muscle mass improves glucose metabolism even at rest, partially buffering the sitting effect.
3. **Anchor movement to existing cues** — before a meeting, after lunch, between calls.

Your Recharge workouts are optimally placed in the workday precisely because of this. A session mid-morning or at lunch breaks the longest sitting stretches and creates a metabolic reset.
