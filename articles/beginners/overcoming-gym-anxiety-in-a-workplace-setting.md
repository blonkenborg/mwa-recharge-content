Gym anxiety is well-documented in commercial gym settings. The workplace gym introduces a specific variant: the people watching are your colleagues, your manager, potentially your direct reports. The stakes feel different.

## The perception gap

The most consistent finding in social anxiety research: people significantly overestimate how much others notice and judge their behaviour. This is called the spotlight effect.

In a gym context, this manifests as: "Everyone is watching me and noticing that I don't know what I'm doing."

Reality: everyone in a gym is primarily focused on their own session. Sustained judgment is rare and usually more neutral than anticipated.

## Practical ways to reduce friction

**Go at less busy times initially.** The first few sessions when everything is new and uncertain are the highest-friction moments. Lower the social exposure during this window.

**Focus on the machines, not the humans.** The EGYM machines guide you through the movement — there's no ambiguity about what to do or how to do it.

**Anchor on the habit, not the performance.** In the first weeks, showing up is the win. You're building an identity, not performing for an audience.

**Find one other person doing it.** If you know a colleague also uses the gym, the behaviour becomes normal for people like you rather than unusual.

## The long view

Gym anxiety is almost universally a front-loaded problem. It diminishes rapidly with repetition. By session 10 or 15, most people report that the anxiety has largely resolved — not because the social dynamics changed, but because the behaviour became part of their identity.
