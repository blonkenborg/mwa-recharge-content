The first month of training is a period of rapid adaptation — but not the kind most people expect. Understanding what's actually happening prevents the frustration that causes most beginners to quit.

## Week 1-2: Neural adaptation (strength without size)

The most common experience in the first two weeks: sessions feel hard, you're sore, and you may not see any physical change.

What's actually happening is primarily neural. Your nervous system is learning to recruit muscle fibres more efficiently. The strength gains in weeks 1-4 come almost entirely from improved motor recruitment, not from actual muscle growth.

This is why beginners often get significantly stronger without looking noticeably different. The muscles are there — the brain is just learning to use them.

Delayed onset muscle soreness (DOMS) peaks 24-48 hours after a session and typically diminishes in subsequent weeks as the muscles adapt.

## Week 3-4: First signs of real adaptation

By week 3-4, the neural adaptations are largely in place. You'll notice the same sessions feel easier, recovery between sessions is faster, and your movement patterns feel more natural.

This is the inflection point where progressive overload becomes important. If the sessions aren't getting harder, the stimulus isn't sufficient for continued adaptation.

## What doesn't happen in 4 weeks

Significant visible muscle growth requires 6-8 weeks minimum. Don't use the mirror as a 4-week metric.

## The right metrics for week 4

- Are sessions feeling more manageable?
- Are you showing up consistently?
- Is the habit becoming more automatic?

If yes to all three: the training is working.
