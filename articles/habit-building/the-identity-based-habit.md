Most approaches to behavior change focus on outcomes ("I want to lose weight") or processes ("I will go to the gym three times a week"). James Clear's framework in *Atomic Habits* identifies a deeper level: **identity**.

## The three levels

1. **Outcome-based**: changing what you have ("I want a better physique")
2. **Process-based**: changing what you do ("I will train three times a week")
3. **Identity-based**: changing what you believe about yourself ("I am someone who trains")

Outcome and process-based changes are fragile because they depend on external motivation. Identity-based changes are self-reinforcing because every action becomes evidence for or against a self-image.

## How identity change works

Identity isn't declared — it's earned through evidence. Each workout completed is a small vote for the identity of "someone who trains." Each skipped session is a vote against it. Over time, the accumulated evidence becomes undeniable.

The goal isn't to achieve a result and then identify as a fit person. The goal is to decide who to become, then prove it with small actions.

## The practical implication

When motivation is low, the question "do I feel like working out?" produces unreliable answers. The question "what would a person who trains consistently do right now?" is almost always clearer.

## How this connects to your training

Every logged workout — however short — is a vote for the identity of someone who takes care of their body. The streak is visible proof that the identity is real.
