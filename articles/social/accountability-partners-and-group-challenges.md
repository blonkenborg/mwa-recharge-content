Telling another person you'll do something is behaviourally different from deciding to do it privately. The mechanism is commitment and consistency — once you've made a public commitment, the psychological cost of not following through increases substantially.

## What the research shows

A study by the American Society of Training and Development found striking differences in completion rates:
- Having a specific idea: 10%
- Making a conscious decision: 25%
- Committing to someone else: 65%
- Setting a specific appointment with someone to report back: 95%

The effect compounds. The more socially embedded the commitment, the more reliable the follow-through.

## Practical accountability structures

**The simplest version:** Tell one colleague you're training on specific days this week. The knowledge that someone is aware changes your behaviour.

**The intermediate version:** Find a training partner with similar availability. Agree to train together 2-3 times per week. Cancelling feels different when it means cancelling on someone else.

**The structured version:** Group challenges in Recharge make training visible across a team. When colleagues can see each other's progress, the social norm of training shifts.

## Why groups outperform individuals

A 2016 study in Preventive Medicine Reports found that people who exercised with a partner showed significantly higher exercise adherence at 12 months — even after controlling for initial motivation levels.

The partner didn't increase motivation. They increased the cost of stopping.
