The most important determinant of whether any individual exercises consistently is not their willpower, their schedule, or their knowledge of training science. It is whether the people around them exercise.

Social norms are the dominant force in health behaviour. Behaviour that is normal for people like me requires essentially no motivation to maintain.

## How culture shifts

Culture doesn't shift through top-down mandates. It shifts through the accumulation of visible individual behaviour.

When one person in an office uses the gym during the workday, it's unusual. When five do, it's a thing that some people do. When fifteen do, it's normal. The visibility of the behaviour matters — which is why the gym's location, the social conversation about it, and the team challenge leaderboard are not peripheral features. They are the culture-building mechanism.

## What leaders can do

**Model the behaviour publicly.** Leaders who train visibly — not performatively, but normally — signal that it's acceptable to spend 20 minutes of the workday on physical health. This permission is more powerful than any wellness communication.

**Normalise talking about it.** "I'm heading to the gym before our 2pm call" creates a different social reality than treating training as something private.

**Use challenges as social infrastructure.** Team challenges in Recharge create a shared context — something to reference, compare, and discuss.

## Your role

You don't have to lead a wellness initiative to shift culture. Every time you show up, you're shifting the reference point for what's normal in your environment.

Your streak isn't just a personal metric. It's environmental data for everyone around you.
