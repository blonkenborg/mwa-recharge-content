Athletes spend thousands on supplements, equipment, and coaching. The single highest-leverage intervention for strength and recovery is free: **sleep**.

## The growth hormone connection

Approximately 70% of daily growth hormone release occurs during slow-wave (deep) sleep, according to research by Van Cauter et al. Growth hormone is the primary anabolic (muscle-building) signal in the body. Cutting sleep short doesn't just leave you tired — it directly reduces the hormonal environment required for adaptation.

## What one week of poor sleep does

A study published in *Sleep* (Spiegel et al.) found that restricting sleep to 5 hours per night for one week reduced testosterone levels in young men by 10–15%. Testosterone is the other key anabolic hormone. The effect was equivalent to aging 10–15 years.

## Performance and injury risk

Sleep deprivation impairs reaction time, strength output, and coordination — the same cognitive and physical resources required for effective training. It also increases injury risk: a study of young athletes found that those sleeping fewer than 8 hours per night were 1.7x more likely to be injured.

## Practical application

- Prioritize 7–9 hours — not as a luxury, but as a training variable
- Keep a consistent sleep schedule, including weekends
- Avoid intense training within 2 hours of sleep (elevates core temperature)
- Treat poor sleep weeks as reduced-capacity training weeks, not failures

## How this connects to your training

If strength test results feel stagnant, sleep quality is the first variable to audit — before changing the training program.
