The EGYM strength test happens every 12 weeks. Most users treat it as a check-in. It's actually the calibration mechanism for everything else.

## What the test measures

The strength test determines your 1-repetition maximum (1RM) — the maximum weight you can move once with correct form — for each machine in the circuit. This is the gold standard metric in resistance training for setting appropriate training loads.

Rather than a single maximum lift (which carries injury risk), the EGYM system estimates your 1RM from a submaximal effort. You lift a challenging but manageable weight to a comfortable limit; the algorithm extrapolates using validated repetition-to-maximal-load equations.

## Why personalised load matters

Training load needs to sit in a specific range relative to your 1RM to drive the adaptation you're targeting:
- **Hypertrophy (muscle growth):** 65-85% of 1RM
- **Strength:** 80-95% of 1RM
- **Endurance/technique:** 50-65% of 1RM

Without knowing your 1RM, the system is guessing. With it, every session can be calibrated to an effective training zone.

## What happens when you skip the test

Your 1RM estimate stays fixed. Your actual capacity grows. The gap between your actual capacity and your programmed load widens — and your sessions progressively move out of the effective training zone.

This is the primary reason consistent trainers plateau while thinking they're doing everything right.

## How to approach the test

Treat it as a training session. Go in rested, warm up at a light load on each machine, then complete the protocol. The better data you give the system, the better the system trains you.
