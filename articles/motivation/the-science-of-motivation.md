Here's an uncomfortable truth: motivation is a feeling, not a resource. It fluctuates with sleep, stress, blood sugar, and a hundred other variables. Relying on it as the primary driver for exercise is a setup for inconsistency.

## Self-determination theory

Decades of research in motivational psychology have identified three core psychological needs that drive sustainable behavior:

1. **Autonomy** — feeling that actions are a personal choice
2. **Competence** — experiencing growth and mastery
3. **Relatedness** — feeling connected to others

When exercise satisfies all three, people stick with it long-term. When it doesn't, they quit.

## What actually drives consistency

The most consistent exercisers aren't the most motivated — they've built systems that don't require motivation:
- **Implementation intentions**: "I will exercise at [time] in [place]" triples follow-through compared to vague intentions (Gollwitzer, 1999).
- **Identity-based habits**: People who say "I am someone who trains" are far more consistent than those who say "I am trying to get fit."
- **Progress visibility**: Tracking small wins activates the reward system and creates intrinsic motivation.

## How this connects to your training

Every day the streak grows, an identity is being reinforced — the identity of someone who shows up. That's not just a metric. It's self-concept in action.
