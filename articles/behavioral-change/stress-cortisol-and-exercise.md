Chronic work stress doesn't just feel bad — it has measurable physiological consequences that directly interfere with fitness progress.

## What cortisol does to the body

**Cortisol** is the primary stress hormone. In short bursts, it's adaptive — it sharpens focus and mobilizes energy. Chronically elevated cortisol is destructive:
- **Catabolic**: breaks down muscle tissue for energy
- **Anabolic suppression**: reduces testosterone and growth hormone
- **Fat storage**: increases visceral fat deposition
- **Sleep disruption**: suppresses melatonin, reducing recovery quality

For anyone trying to build or maintain muscle, chronic stress is a direct antagonist.

## Exercise as cortisol regulation

Acute exercise temporarily elevates cortisol, but the net effect is strongly positive. Regular strength training:
- Reduces baseline cortisol levels (Rimmele et al., 2007)
- Improves the HPA axis response, meaning less cortisol is released in response to psychological stressors
- Increases BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor), which improves stress resilience

## The timing consideration

Very high-intensity training when already significantly stressed can compound cortisol load. This is one reason moderate, consistent sessions often outperform sporadic high-intensity efforts for stressed office workers — the hormonal environment is more conducive to recovery.

## How this connects to your training

Short strength sessions during the workday aren't just exercise breaks — they actively regulate the hormonal environment created by desk work and meetings. The workout is the medicine.
