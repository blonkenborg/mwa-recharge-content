There's a persistent myth in fitness culture: that the harder and more extreme the session, the better the results. The science tells a different story.

## Frequency matters more than intensity

A meta-analysis published in the *Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research* (Ralston et al., 2017) found that **training frequency** — how often muscles are stimulated per week — is a stronger predictor of hypertrophy than session intensity or volume in any single session.

In practical terms: training a muscle group twice a week with moderate effort beats one brutal session followed by five days of recovery.

## The compounding effect of showing up

Consistency produces a compounding return. Someone who exercises four times a week for a year completes 208 sessions. Someone who does two intense sessions per week completes 104. The volume gap — and the adaptation gap — is enormous, even if each individual session looks similar.

## Injury risk and the intensity trap

High-intensity sessions carry higher injury risk. Injuries don't just pause progress — they often derail habits entirely. A consistent, moderate approach has a dramatically better long-term completion rate than a feast-or-famine intensity model.

## How this connects to your training

The Recharge model is deliberately designed around frequency. Each short session counts. Each streak day is a compounding unit of adaptation. Showing up consistently is the strategy — not saving it all for one heroic session.
