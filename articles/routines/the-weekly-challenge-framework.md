There's a reason most effective fitness programs — and the Recharge challenge — are structured around weekly targets rather than daily ones.

## The problem with daily targets

Daily targets are binary: hit or miss. A meeting that runs long, a sick child, an unexpected work deadline — any of these breaks the chain and triggers the psychological failure response. The all-or-nothing framing of daily targets produces either rigidity or abandonment.

## Weekly targets create flexibility without lowering the bar

A weekly target of 3 training sessions can be achieved in an infinite number of daily configurations. Missed Monday? Still achievable. Busy Wednesday? Shift to Thursday. The target remains the same; only the distribution changes.

Research on **goal flexibility** shows that maintaining the same overall target while allowing distribution flexibility significantly increases long-term completion rates (Fishbach & Dhar, 2005).

## The recovery day built in

Weekly targets naturally build in rest. Even a weekly goal of 5 sessions leaves two days for recovery, adaptation, and life. This isn't laziness — it's physiology. Muscles adapt during rest, not during training.

## Accountability without rigidity

The weekly structure creates accountability over a meaningful time horizon without creating the fragility of daily obligation. It's the difference between a goal that responds to real life and one that breaks against it.

## How this connects to your training

The Recharge weekly challenge is designed precisely on this framework. The number of sessions targets the physiologically optimal training frequency while remaining achievable across the full range of real workweeks.
