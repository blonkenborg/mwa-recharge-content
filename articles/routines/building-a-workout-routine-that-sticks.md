Most workout routines fail not because people lack discipline — they fail because the routine was designed wrong from the start.

## The design flaw in most routines

The typical approach: pick an ambitious goal, design a demanding schedule, start with high motivation, struggle when life intervenes, quit. The failure point is almost always the gap between the ideal schedule and the real schedule.

## Design for the worst day, not the best

**Sustainable routines are built around the minimum viable version, not the ideal version.** The question to ask isn't "what's the best workout I could do?" — it's "what's the workout I can do even on the worst day of the week?"

If the answer on a hard day is "15 minutes and 3 machines," that's the routine. Better sessions will happen naturally on easier days.

## Environment design

The biggest predictor of exercise compliance isn't motivation — it's proximity. Research by Wansink and others in behavioral economics shows that making a behavior physically easier increases follow-through dramatically. Having workout gear accessible, having the app already open, removing friction at every step.

## Implementation intentions

Stating exactly when and where a workout will happen — not just "I'll exercise more" — triples follow-through according to research by Gollwitzer and Sheeran. "I will train at 12:30 in the gym on the ground floor on Monday, Wednesday, and Friday" is far more effective than a vague intention.

## How this connects to your training

The Recharge weekly challenge is a built-in routine scaffold. The target is achievable even on busy weeks. The streak creates accountability. The rest is environment design and specificity.
