The most effective technique for building exercise consistency isn't a complex program or a motivational speech. It's a rule so simple it almost sounds trivial: **never do less than two minutes**.

## The psychology behind the rule

James Clear describes this in *Atomic Habits* as "the two-minute rule." The idea is that any new habit should take less than two minutes to start. The goal isn't the two-minute workout — it's showing up. The act of starting rewires the brain to associate the cue (changing shoes, walking to the gym floor) with action rather than resistance.

## Activation energy

Behavioral scientists call the effort required to start a task **activation energy**. High activation energy is the enemy of consistency. By committing to just two minutes — just walking to the machine and logging in — the activation energy drops to near zero. Once started, finishing naturally follows.

## The identity angle

Each time the two-minute rule is honored, even on the hardest days, it sends a vote to the identity of "someone who trains." Over weeks and months, those votes accumulate into an unambiguous self-image.

## How this connects to your training

On days when motivation is low, the goal isn't a great session. The goal is simply to show up and start. The streak reflects that. Even a single set logged is a kept commitment to yourself.
