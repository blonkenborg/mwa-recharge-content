A common misconception is that more training always means more progress. In reality, training is the stimulus — recovery is where adaptation happens.

## What happens after a workout

For 24–72 hours after a strength session, muscles are in a state of repair. Satellite cells (muscle stem cells) fuse with damaged fibers and rebuild them with greater cross-sectional area. This process requires protein, sleep, hydration, and time.

## Signs of insufficient recovery

- Performance declining despite consistent effort
- Persistent soreness that doesn't resolve in 48 hours
- Poor sleep or elevated resting heart rate
- Loss of motivation to train (a surprisingly reliable indicator)

## The most effective recovery tools

1. **Sleep** — growth hormone is primarily released during deep sleep. 7–9 hours is non-negotiable for muscle recovery.
2. **Protein** — 1.6–2.2g per kg of body weight per day is the evidence-based range for muscle protein synthesis (Morton et al., 2018).
3. **Active recovery** — light movement on rest days increases blood flow and accelerates clearance of metabolic waste products.
4. **Stress management** — chronic psychological stress elevates cortisol, which is catabolic. Managing stress is as important as managing training load.

## How this connects to your training

One benefit of the Recharge model is that short sessions at moderate intensity are far easier to recover from than long, high-intensity sessions. This is exactly why consistent daily training is achievable.
