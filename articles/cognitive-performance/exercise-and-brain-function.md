When you exercise, you are not just conditioning muscle and cardiovascular tissue. You are directly stimulating the brain.

## Immediate effects

Within a single session, exercise triggers the release of several neuroactive compounds:

**BDNF (Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor)** — often called "fertiliser for the brain." BDNF promotes the growth and maintenance of neurons, improves synaptic plasticity, and is sharply elevated for 1-2 hours after exercise.

**Dopamine and serotonin** — both are involved in mood regulation, focus, and motivation. Exercise is one of the most reliable non-pharmacological ways to elevate both simultaneously.

**Norepinephrine** — improves attention and alertness. The post-workout focus window is largely driven by norepinephrine.

## Structural changes over time

Regular exercise is associated with measurable increases in hippocampal volume — the brain region responsible for memory consolidation. One landmark study found that 12 months of aerobic exercise increased hippocampal volume by 2%, reversing age-related decline.

Strength training specifically is associated with improved prefrontal cortex function — the area responsible for planning, decision-making, and impulse control.

## Practical implication

The 20 minutes you spend training is an investment with a 2-4 hour cognitive return. Post-workout is not the time to do passive tasks. It's when your brain is at its sharpest — schedule demanding cognitive work for this window.
