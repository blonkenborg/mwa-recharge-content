Most people who train consistently for years make slow progress not because they train incorrectly, but because of a small number of systematic errors that compound over time.

## 1. Training at the same intensity indefinitely

The body adapts to training stimuli. Once it has adapted, the same stimulus produces diminishing returns. Progress requires progressive overload — incrementally increasing the challenge over time.

**Fix:** Track performance. If you're not lifting more, doing more reps, or completing the same work with less effort, something needs to change.

## 2. Insufficient recovery between sessions

Muscle protein synthesis peaks within 24 hours of a session and remains elevated for 36-48 hours. Training the same muscle group before it has recovered interrupts the adaptation process.

**Fix:** 48 hours minimum between sessions targeting the same muscle groups.

## 3. Neglecting compound movements

Isolation exercises have their place. But compound movements — squat, hinge, press, pull, row — produce disproportionately greater hormonal response and involve more muscle mass.

**Fix:** Anchor each session to at least one compound movement. The EGYM circuit is designed around this principle.

## 4. Underestimating the intensity required

The last rep of each set should require maximum effort. If you're stopping because time is up, not because the reps are hard, the intensity is too low.

## 5. Treating every missed session as a failure

Consistency over months and years matters far more than perfection in any given week. Missing a session is noise. Missing three weeks is a signal. Act on the signal, not the noise.
