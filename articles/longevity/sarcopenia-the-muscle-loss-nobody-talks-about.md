Sarcopenia is the age-related loss of skeletal muscle mass and function. It begins earlier than most people expect — measurable decline starts in the third decade of life — and accelerates sharply after 60.

The average rate of muscle loss without intervention: 3-8% per decade from age 30, accelerating to up to 15% per decade after 70.

## Why it matters

Muscle mass is not just an aesthetic variable. It is the primary reservoir for glucose disposal (critical for metabolic health), the foundation of mobility and fall prevention, and a significant determinant of all-cause mortality.

Grip strength — one of the most accessible proxies for whole-body muscle strength — is consistently one of the top predictors of longevity in large epidemiological studies, independent of other health markers.

## What causes sarcopenia to accelerate

1. **Sedentary behaviour** — muscle requires mechanical stimulation to maintain itself. Disuse atrophy is the dominant driver of early sarcopenia.
2. **Insufficient protein intake** — anabolic resistance increases with age, requiring higher protein intakes to produce the same muscle protein synthesis effect.
3. **Hormonal changes** — declining testosterone and growth hormone reduce the anabolic signal to muscle tissue.

## What intervenes most effectively

Resistance training is the single most effective intervention. Multiple trials in elderly populations have demonstrated meaningful muscle mass and function gains from progressive strength training. The adaptive capacity never disappears — it just requires more stimulus.

Starting at 35 is better than starting at 55. Starting at 55 is better than starting at 70. Every strength session you complete is a direct counter to the most predictable health decline of ageing.
