At 25, strength training mostly affects how you look and feel. The health stakes are real but distant. At 45, 55, or 65, the equation shifts significantly.

## What's happening in the body after 40

**Hormonal shift:** Testosterone and growth hormone decline gradually from the late 20s, accelerating in the 40s. Strength training is one of the most potent non-pharmacological ways to maintain anabolic hormonal tone.

**Mitochondrial decline:** Muscle cells become less efficient at energy production with age. Strength training stimulates mitochondrial biogenesis, creating new mitochondria and partially reversing this process.

**Bone density:** Peak bone density occurs around age 30. After that, the balance tips toward resorption. Resistance training is the most powerful non-pharmacological stimulus for bone formation, directly reducing osteoporosis risk.

**Metabolic slowdown:** Resting metabolic rate declines with age largely because of muscle loss, not the ageing process per se. Maintaining muscle mass largely maintains metabolic rate.

## The compounding logic

Consider two 45-year-olds: one starts training consistently at 45 and maintains it; the other starts at 65. At 75, the first has 30 years of accumulated adaptation. The second has 10. The gap in strength, bone density, balance, and metabolic health is not a 3x difference — it compounds far beyond that.

## What adequate looks like

Consistent resistance training three times per week — with progressive overload — is sufficient to maintain and build lean mass at any age. The bar is achievable. The consequence of not meeting it is significant and concrete.
